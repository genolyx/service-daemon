Methods and Limitations

Pharmacogenomic analysis is performed using PharmCAT (Pharmacogenomics Clinical Annotation Tool), which matches variant calls to known pharmacogenomic allele definitions and generates phenotype predictions based on published CPIC (Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium) and DPWG (Dutch Pharmacogenetics Working Group) guidelines.

Genomic DNA obtained from the submitted sample is enriched for targeted regions using a hybridization-based protocol, and sequenced using Illumina technology. Variants within pharmacogenes are called using standard bioinformatics pipelines and translated into star-allele diplotypes by PharmCAT. Activity scores and predicted phenotypes are assigned according to the relevant CPIC or DPWG gene-specific tables. Drug-gene interaction recommendations follow published clinical guidelines.

Limitations: This test may not detect all known pharmacogenomic variants. Rare or novel alleles not cataloged in PharmCAT's allele definition files will not be identified. Structural variants, gene deletions, duplications, and hybrid alleles (e.g., CYP2D6 gene conversions) may not be fully resolved by short-read sequencing. Activity scores and phenotype predictions are based on currently available evidence and may be updated as new data emerge. This test does not account for drug-drug interactions, non-genetic factors (age, weight, organ function, concomitant medications), or epigenetic modifications that may influence drug metabolism. Results should be interpreted by a qualified healthcare professional in the context of the patient's complete clinical picture.

A negative or "normal" result does not guarantee a normal drug response. Pharmacogenomic testing is one component of personalized medicine and should be used alongside standard clinical decision-making.
